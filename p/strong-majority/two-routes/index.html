Two Routes to Five Colors

11 July 2026 · companion to the project page

The theorem

Every admissible graph admits a strong majority edge-coloring with five colors.

A strong majority edge-coloring assigns a color to every edge so that, for each edge, no single color covers more than half of the edges adjacent to it; a graph is admissible if it has no degree-1 vertex adjacent to a degree-2 vertex (the one locally impossible configuration). The theorem was first proved by Antoniuk, Prorok, and Salia (arXiv:2607.00212, 30 June 2026). On 11 July 2026 it was verified by the Lean 4 kernel — via a proof with a different architecture — as SMaj.Synthesis.maj_le_five, with axiom audit exactly [propext, Classical.choice, Quot.sound]. Theorem — kernel-checked.

This page is about the two architectures, and then about how the second one was found, stress-tested, broken, repaired, raced, and checked. Every number below is auditable against the released artifact and working records.

The short version, if you read nothing else: the published proof runs on equitable colorings and ear surgery; the verified proof runs on chain contraction and Vizing's theorem. The verified proof was assembled in one working day on top of previously kernel-checked infrastructure, by three different AI proof systems racing on five frozen lemmas, under a standing audit that accepted nothing without an independent rebuild. Along the way the audit refuted one of our own written claims — the assertion that this entire route was impossible — with a six-vertex kernel-checked counterexample. The final theorem was verified twice, in two clean-room builds of 8,037 compilation jobs each.

Route A and Route B

Route A (the paper). Antoniuk–Prorok–Salia prove an equitable-coloring theorem (after Hilton and de Werra): if no vertex degree is divisible by k, the graph has a k-edge-coloring in which every vertex sees any two colors almost equally often. Their five-color proof performs five graph reductions — removing loop-shaped ears, ears anchored at degree-five vertices, length-two ears, long ears, and finally attaching a leaf wherever the degree is a multiple of five — applies the equitable theorem with k = 5 to the trimmed graph, then undoes the five reductions in reverse, recoloring locally each time. Degree-five vertices, made temporarily degree-four by the ear removals, end up with a free fifth color that pays for the restorations.

Route B (the verified proof). Decompose the graph into chains — maximal paths whose interior vertices have degree 2 — running between junctions (vertices of degree ≥ 3). At each junction of degree d, partition the d edge-ends into at most ⌈d/4⌉ groups of size at most 4, steering group sizes to at most 3 whenever the arithmetic allows (it fails to allow exactly at d = 4, 7, 8, 10, 11, …). Contract each chain of length ≤ 2 to a single edge between its end-groups, producing an auxiliary multigraph M of maximum degree 4. Simplify M by two local rules (un-contract one short chain in each doubled pair between full groups; set aside the remaining parallel classes) until what is left, M₀, is a simple graph of maximum degree 4. Then the single deep input: Vizing's theorem — a simple graph of maximum degree 4 has a proper 5-edge-coloring. Reinsert the set-aside parallel edges greedily (a counting inequality guarantees room), color the two ports of each long chain independently, fill chain interiors by a list-avoidance lemma, and transport everything back to the original graph. A four-condition interface (a "glue coloring": group-rainbow, group-count, chain-end, and interior conditions) had already been proved — in this program's earlier six-color formalization — to imply the strong majority property. Route B's entire new mathematics is: build a five-color glue coloring.

The two routes share their hard core. An analysis during the work showed that any equitable-coloring statement strong enough at degree-4 vertices contains Vizing-at-Δ=4 (equitability at degree 4 with five colors forces the incident color counts 1,1,1,1,0 — local properness), so neither route dodges that difficulty; they package it differently. Route A buys it inside the Hilton–de Werra theorem; Route B pays for it directly and reuses previously verified plumbing for everything else.

Finding Route B

The starting position on the morning of 11 July: the program had a complete kernel-checked proof that six colors suffice, built around the glue-coloring interface; a same-morning formalization of a recoloring toolkit (rotation and color-swap lemmas after Kierstead's method — to our knowledge the first formalization of Kierstead-path machinery in any proof assistant — closed after a focused session in which the winning proof took about 90 minutes); and a census computation. The six-color proof's own palette came from Shannon's multigraph bound, which is exactly tight at 6 on "fat triangles" — triples of junctions pairwise joined by doubled edges — so the six-color architecture could not be pushed to five as it stood.

Two things happened in the first hours, one of them embarrassing in a useful way.

The refutation attempt came up empty. Before spending effort proving the theorem, we tried to falsify it. An exhaustive computation over all 268,478 admissible graphs on at most 9 vertices found a four-coloring for every single one (plus exhaustive checks of all 89,771 four-regular graphs on 10, 12, and 14 vertices). No admissible graph requiring five colors — let alone six — has ever been observed. Exact computation. This killed the counterexample scenario and, incidentally, is living evidence for the still-open four-color conjecture.

Our own impossibility claim was refuted at the kernel. The six-color artifact's documentation asserted that the glue route could not produce a five-color witness — the fat-triangle obstruction, stated as fact. During route analysis, one review track challenged this and produced a six-vertex candidate: a fat triangle whose doubled edges are chains. The corresponding five-color glue coloring was checked by the Lean kernel (decide, standard axioms) against the frozen definitions — on the very configuration the documentation declared impossible. The impossibility was a true fact about one construction (Shannon's), written up as a fact about the route. The claim had never been a theorem; it was a comment. Protocol changed the same hour: impossibility claims now require a formal declaration name plus axiom audit, or an explicit "(prose, unverified)" tag. The reopened glue route became the main line, and everything that follows happened on it.

Formalization-grade reading surfaced three clarifications to the paper's argument. In parallel, one track transcribed the Antoniuk–Prorok–Salia proof step by step, to cost out a faithful formalization of Route A. The theorem survives unaffected; the transcription found three points in the written argument that need repair or tidying. First (minor): the displayed inequality in the proof of the paper's Theorem 4 — the four-color result for graphs with no degree-2 or degree-4 vertices — fails at degree-1 vertices; the conclusion stands, because a one-line count using the bound only at the other endpoint restores it (the authors handle exactly this degree-1 subtlety in their five-color proof, but the corresponding step is omitted in Theorem 4's). Second (editorial): the proof of the paper's Claim 10 contains two consecutive drafts of the same "Case k = 1" paragraph. Third, the substantive one: in undoing the first reduction (R1), the written proof specifies how to restore a degree-5 vertex that lost one loop ear, but a degree-5 vertex carrying two loop ears — two triangles sharing the vertex, plus one pendant edge; a legitimate admissible configuration — finishes the reduction phase holding three auxiliary leaves, a state neither restoration paragraph covers. A repair using only the paper's own tools (its Claim 8 balance property and the distinctness argument of its Remark 9, feeding its Claim 10 extension) closes the case. Before release, all three claims were handed to a second, hostile referee instructed to kill them, working directly from the hash-pinned arXiv v1 LaTeX source. All three were confirmed — and the hostile pass caught an error of ours: the internal notes had mis-numbered the paper's results (placing the duplicated paragraph in "Claim 7" rather than Claim 10, among other slips), corrected before anything shipped or was sent to the authors. Confirmed by adversarial internal review against the arXiv v1 source; prose mathematics, not kernel-checked. The authors were notified privately, with the repair, before this page went live.

The route survived an adversarial battery before any proving began. A dedicated adversarial track built 14 explicit nasty instances — fat-triangle variants, degree-4-rich graphs, greedy-jamming configurations — with an exact checker that verifies a proposed coloring in under a second. The first version of Route B failed 3 of its 12 applicable checks, including one certified case gap (a chain-transport case that random testing triggered on roughly a third of graphs). The repair adopted a hybrid of two competing route drafts: monochromatic transport for length-2 chains, independently colored ports for longer ones. The repaired route passed all 15 checks of the extended battery, and its structural invariants were machine-verified on 1,854 random graphs with zero failures. Only then was formalization money spent.

Five lemmas, three provers, one audit

Route B was frozen as five Lean statements against the unchanged six-color artifact — the "ladder":

ContentAssessed difficulty at freeze
Λ1Grouping arithmetic: d splits into ⌈d/4⌉ parts of size ≤ 4, size ≤ 3 when d ∈ {3, 5}trivial
Λ2Vizing's theorem at Δ ≤ 4, five colors, in the artifact's multigraph encodingthe deep input; no mathlib support
Λ3Greedy reinsertion of parallel classesmoderate
Λ4The construction: build the five-color glue coloringthe largest surface
Λ5Final assembly: Λ4 plus the banked interface dispatch gives the theoremone line

Statement fidelity was absolute throughout: the target maj_le_five stayed byte-identical to its months-old pin, all definitions frozen, and every candidate proof was diffed token-for-token against the pins before acceptance.

Predictions were registered before results came back. Before committing to the ladder, six smaller infrastructure pieces were sent to Harmonic's Aristotle (an automated theorem-proving system) with written acceptance probabilities, logged in advance:

Predicted number of successes: 3.05. Actual: 4. The ranking was perfect — everything rated ≥ 0.45 succeeded, everything ≤ 0.15 failed — but the mid-range predictions were systematically pessimistic, and the recalibration was written down before the next wave. The two failures were the most informative returns of the six: both independently hit the same missing ingredient — the existence of Eulerian trails, which mathlib currently lacks (it is an explicit TODO in mathlib's source) — and both independently derived that a divisibility hypothesis the task header speculated was removable is in fact necessary (witness: K₅ at k = 4; a triangle at k = 2). Two blind runs, same wall, same correction. That settled the architecture question: the equitable-coloring route would cost a from-scratch Euler theory; the Vizing route would not. Pre-registered predictions vs. outcomes; all logged before returns.

The race. The five ladder statements then went to three systems in parallel — Aristotle (eight submissions across framings), an OpenAI reasoning model, and an Anthropic reasoning model, the latter two iterating compile-and-fix in pinned environments — with a standing rule: the first kernel-accepted proof of each statement wins, and nothing counts until a separate audit step independently rebuilds it from source and prints its axioms. The final scoreboard:

  • Λ1 — closed twice, independently, within the hour: Aristotle produced a 42-line proof by explicit closed-form witness (no induction); the OpenAI model produced a 38-line strong-induction proof. Same statement, two structurally different proof objects. (Pre-registered 0.97.)
  • Λ2 — the bottleneck, and everyone knew it: five independent proof attempts on other lemmas each named Vizing-at-Δ≤4 as their single missing prerequisite. (Mathlib has no edge-coloring machinery at all; the one existing Lean formalization of Vizing's theorem — Bhoja, arXiv:2512.13999, Dec 2025 — lives on a bespoke graph type incompatible with this artifact's encoding, so the theorem had to be proved in-file.) It fell to the Anthropic model: 864 lines, a fan-rotation induction powered by the recoloring kit that had been kernel-checked that same morning. The instructive detail: two parallel attempts given progressively less supporting context both reduced to the same final extension step and stopped exactly there. Three attempts, three context levels, strictly monotone progress in context — on a hard named subproblem, what you arm the prover with matters more than how often you retry it.
  • Λ3 — Aristotle proved the reinsertion content outright (its other half was Λ2, so Λ2's closure made it unconditional).
  • Λ4 — the centerpiece, closed by composition: the OpenAI model built the construction core (the auxiliary-graph combinatorics, the traversal and dispatch engine), while Aristotle submissions returned complementary proved components with near-zero overlap — an interior-fill pass, the chain-end transport layer, and, notably, a uniform grouping construction that eliminated one of the two existential witnesses from the statement: it proved that a suitable grouping can be manufactured from any coloring satisfying three row-level properties, so the target provably shrank mid-race from "construct a grouping and a coloring" to "construct a coloring." (The pre-registered chances that a single first-wave submission would close Λ4 outright: 0.10–0.15 each, ≈ 0.35 that at least one would. None did — those probabilities were honest — but their proved partials, rated 0.60–0.75, all landed.)
  • Λ5 — one line, as predicted (0.95), the moment Λ4 existed.

The endgame was three checks. After the merge of everything above, the unproved content of the whole theorem had shrunk to three verification lemmas: that the fully assembled coloring satisfies the multiplicity cap, interior distinctness, and the chain-end condition. Pre-registered: ≈ 0.70 that the next prover wave closes all three. In the event, the in-session tracks got there first, twice: the Anthropic model completed a verification block over the OpenAI model's construction, and the OpenAI model independently completed its own verification block. Two finished proofs of the same frozen statement, sharing the construction skeleton but with independently produced verification layers.

The double gate. Each completion then went through the full acceptance ritual, separately: token-identity of the statement against every pin; a scan of the sources (zero sorry, zero hits on fourteen proof-subversion patterns, exactly one public declaration per file); a from-scratch rebuild in a fresh clone of the pinned environment — 8,037 compilation jobs, exit code 0, once per completion in two separate clean environments; and axiom prints across the whole dependency chain, every one exactly [propext, Classical.choice, Quot.sound], zero sorryAx. Decision to attempt the theorem: about 15:45. Second independent kernel confirmation: about 19:25 the same day — three hours and forty minutes, standing on a base of previously verified infrastructure that took considerably longer.

Negative results and incidents, for the record

  • The first-wave ladder predictions that failed, failed honestly. Full closure in the first prover wave was pre-registered at ≈ 0.25 and did not happen; no single Λ4 submission closed it (≈ 0.35 that one would). The race was won by merge and iteration, not by a lottery ticket.
  • One delivery arrived corrupted. Mid-race, one agent-delivered Lean file contained a literal "…440 tokens truncated…" marker — a transport-layer corruption that would have been catastrophic if trusted. It was caught immediately by the standing discipline that nothing counts unless it rebuilds from disk in a fresh environment; the file was re-emitted and hash-verified, and hash verification of every file delivery was adopted on the spot.
  • A stale comment propagated a refuted claim. One late prover run, given a file whose documentation still contained the kernel-refuted impossibility claim, reproduced that claim in its diagnosis; two others working from the same file did not. The lesson was mechanical, not moral: refuted claims get patched at the source file, because downstream readers — human or machine — inherit comments as facts.
  • A same-day comparison of proving systems, same task, identical input: the morning's recoloring crux was closed by the OpenAI model in about 90 minutes and by Aristotle — independently, with a structurally different proof — in 7 hours 21 minutes of prover time. Both proofs kernel-check. Variance between systems on identical formal tasks is large and real.

What remains open

The four-color conjecture of Kalinowski, Kamyczura, Pilśniak, and Woźniak — Maj′(G) ≤ 4 for every admissible graph (Conjecture 14 of arXiv:2605.23828) — is open, and the bound 4 would be optimal. The census computed along the way is direct evidence for it: among all 268,478 admissible graphs on at most nine vertices, none needs five colors — none has ever been observed anywhere — and the smallest graph needing four is the paw, a triangle with one pendant edge. Exact computation; the conjecture itself is open. The verified five-color machinery was deliberately built with the palette size as a parameter wherever that cost nothing, so parts of it are reusable by whoever — human or machine — takes the next step down.
