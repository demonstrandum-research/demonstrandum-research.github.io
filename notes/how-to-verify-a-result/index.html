How to verify a result

John Erlbacher · 10 July 2026

Demonstrandum's proofs, certificates, and exact computations — and the trust each one requires.

Every Demonstrandum result names its verification artifact. This guide gives the check command, guarantee, and limitations for each class, roughly ordered by the size of its trusted base. Use the heading that matches the grade on the result page.

1. Kernel-checked Lean proofs

A kernel-checked Lean proof is our highest verification grade. The entire claim — definitions, lemmas, theorem — is written in Lean 4 and checked by Lean’s kernel, a small trusted core that verifies every inference down to the axioms of the logic. When we say a theorem is kernel-checked, we mean:

  • the build is sorry-free — no step is assumed;
  • the axiom audit reports exactly propext, Classical.choice, and Quot.sound, the standard Lean/mathlib axioms used by this development. It reports neither sorryAx (the marker of an assumed step) nor native_decide (which would extend trust to the compiler);
  • the definitions are exercised inside the kernel against brute-force enumeration for small parameters. Small-parameter checks test that the definitions behave as intended; the reading kit documents the bridge to the informal claim;
  • a one-page reading kit accompanies the proof. It includes the Lean theorem, an English glossary of its definitions, the bridge from the source claim to the formal statement, the axiom audit, and the rebuild command.

Install the pinned Lean toolchain with elan, then run:

lake build
lake env lean scripts/AxiomCheck.lean

The second command prints the axioms actually used by the main theorem. This confirms the formal theorem against the reported axioms, independently of how the proof was produced.

What it does not establish: that the formal statement matches the informal claim. The reading kit addresses this by placing the source conjecture beside the Lean statement.

2. Independently checkable SAT certificates (DRAT/LRAT)

When a result rests on an exhaustive computation — “no configuration of this kind exists below this bound” — a solver verdict alone is not the artifact. Modern SAT solvers emit proof logs (DRAT/LRAT): step-by-step derivations of unsatisfiability that an independent program can check. We archive these and check them with cake_lpr, a checker that is itself formally verified (in CakeML/HOL4, down to machine code). To check a cell:

cake_lpr <cell>.cnf <cell>.lrat
# expect: s VERIFIED UNSAT

For a satisfiable instance, the certificate is an explicit example checked by a short independent program. Before archival, two independent SAT engines solve both halves of every nontrivial cell.

What it does not establish: that the CNF encoding means what we say. For that, the release documents the encoding, and small cells are cross-checked against direct exhaustive enumeration by independent code.

3. Exact interval-arithmetic certificates

Analytic inequalities (“this two-variable function is nonnegative on the square”) are certified by outward-rounded interval arithmetic over an explicit finite subdivision, with exact symbolic identities at the touching points. Re-running the certifying script reproduces the subdivision, box count, and verdict at any precision at or above the stated one.

Internal review repeats the proof with a different interval library and subdivision. Mutation tests inject a deliberately false constant and confirm that the verifier rejects it.

What it does not establish: kernel-grade certainty. These results are graded as computer-assisted analytic theorems. If a verifier accepts a mutated false claim, the defect blocks release and is logged on the result page until fixed.

4. Dual independently implemented exact computations

Other finite claims — counts, record witnesses, tables — are checked by two independent implementations, in different languages where practical. Both use exact arithmetic (integers and rationals, never floats), agree over the full claimed range, and pass mutation tests.

5. Internally reviewed written proofs

Some results rely on written proofs subjected to independent internal review. Where applicable, mechanizable lemmas are checked beyond the claimed parameter range. These receive a separate prose-proof grade — never the kernel grade — and any incomplete formalization is identified explicitly.

The grades reflect their trusted base. A Lean-kernel result can be checked on your machine against a small, well-studied core; a reviewed prose result depends on its review process, and the page says so. If an artifact fails to reproduce, please contact John Erlbacher.
